01

The problem

Unstructured records create gaps the plan can't close

Health plans using UST Healthproof's risk adjustment platform face a persistent problem: medical records arrive as PDFs, scanned documents, and handwritten notes — unstructured, inconsistent, and time-consuming to review manually. When chronic conditions go undocumented or uncoded, HCC risk scores are incomplete, leading to plan mismatch, CMS audit exposure, and members enrolled in the wrong coverage.

The problem compounds across the care pipeline. Coders miss conditions buried in chart notes. Enrollment analysts make tier decisions on incomplete data. Providers walk into visits without a clinical picture of what's already known about the patient.

"The gap between what's in a member's records and what's in their risk score is where plans lose accuracy, revenue, and member trust simultaneously."

02

Research

Primary and secondary

For primary research, I would conduct structured interviews with medical coders and care managers in the risk adjustment workflow, observing their interactions with Enrollment+™ to surface friction points, cognitive load triggers, and workaround behaviours.

For secondary research, I reviewed CMS-HCC risk adjustment model documentation and OIG audit reports on risk adjustment data validation to understand the regulatory and financial stakes of coding inaccuracies. I also drew on AHIMA's clinical documentation improvement guidelines and UST Healthproof's published case studies on retrospective review outcomes.

03

Users

Three actors, one pipeline

ClearChart serves three distinct roles across the payer and provider ecosystem. Each has a different relationship to the data, a different definition of success, and a different tolerance for complexity in the interface.

Primary · Payer-side
Medical coder / risk adjustment analyst

Reviews AI-flagged conditions, accepts or corrects HCC codes, closes gaps for CMS submission. High volume, compliance-critical, needs speed and trust.

Secondary · Payer-side
Enrollment ops analyst

Uses confirmed HCC data to assign risk tiers and match members to appropriate plans. Needs accurate segmentation, not raw clinical detail.

Tertiary · Provider-side
Physician / care provider

Sees a pre-visit briefing with clinical context — conditions to confirm, open care gaps. Needs clinical framing, not billing language.

05

Design decisions

Choices worth explaining

Five decisions shaped the system's character — each one a deliberate answer to a tension between competing design values.

  • Confidence shown as label and percentage, never percentage alone
    A coder reviewing 40 charts a day can't parse numbers under time pressure. Pairing "High · 94%" with a colour-coded pill lets them scan vertically and act without reading every value.
  • Evidence snippets are non-negotiable on every flagged condition
    Without the source text, the AI is a black box. Showing the exact phrase that triggered each flag turns the system into a reference tool — the right power dynamic for certified coders who will not accept unjustified outputs.
  • Override friction is calibrated to AI confidence, not applied uniformly
    A high-confidence override warrants a required reason — the disagreement is statistically significant. A needs-review override warrants optional friction. Treating both the same would frustrate coders on flags the AI didn't trust either.
  • Gap reasons map to organisational ownership, not just status
    "Missing record" is a provider outreach problem. "Insufficient documentation" is a CDI problem. "Document quality failure" is an ops or IT problem. Naming reasons this way makes each gap immediately routable to the right team.
  • ICD-10 codes are hidden from providers entirely
    The moment a provider sees coding language, the interaction reads as billing. Showing condition names only keeps the frame clinical — and the provider's attention on the patient, not the plan.
