Portfolio case study · Goodreads

Redesigning reading into a habit worth keeping

A research-driven, speculative redesign of Goodreads — shifting the product from a social-first directory to a reading-first companion that respects how people actually track books.

Conceptual project for portfolio purposes. Not affiliated with or endorsed by Goodreads.

01

The problems

Two gaps where Goodreads quietly loses its users

Goodreads has a loyalty problem masked as a discovery problem. The real friction isn't that users can't find books — it's that the product makes it hard to stay connected to the ones they're already reading, and buries the actions they return for most.

Problem 01

A social-first homepage that leads with "Find Friends" over any reading action — making the core use case feel secondary from the first screen.

Problem 02

No quick way to add or organise books, and no sense of reading momentum — making the tracking experience feel like admin rather than habit.

"I have not noticed a single improvement in years. The interface looks the same as it did a decade ago."

— Goodreads user, Reddit

"Being able to quickly add books is entirely absent. It should be the most obvious thing."

— Goodreads user, Reddit

Research

The voice of the user, unsolicited

Rather than formal interviews, I turned to the spaces where Goodreads users speak honestly and at length — App Store reviews and Reddit threads. These sources surfaced unfiltered frustrations: patterns that repeat across hundreds of posts, unprompted, from users who care enough to complain.

Screens

Four surfaces redesigned around the reading act

Each screen targets a specific moment of friction — from the first thing users see, to the micro-interaction of logging a reading update.

The current design greets returning users with a social prompt — "Find your friends" — even when they have books actively in progress. The redesign flips the hierarchy: a personalised welcome surfaces the user's currently reading list immediately, with visual progress bars and contextual update actions. Discovery and social features move below the fold, becoming secondary to the primary reading workflow.

Screen 02 My Books tab — redesigned navigation and CTAs
[ My Books before/after — drop export here ]

The current design presents shelves as a flat, text-heavy list — with "Update your reading progress" as the only visible action, buried above the fold. The redesign replaces this with rich shelf cards: book cover collages, clear counts, and a "Create New Shelf" CTA that gives users agency from the first tap. Scanning your library becomes visual, not administrative.

Screen 03 Currently Reading shelf — progress and quick add
[ Currently Reading before/after — drop export here ]

The current design fills the shelf with publishing metadata — ratings, publication dates, page counts — none of which helps a reader pick up where they left off. The redesign strips this back to what matters: progress bars showing percentage and page position, an inline "Update" action per book, and an "Add Books" CTA above the fold. Reading momentum is now visible at a glance.

Screen 04 Update screen — progress logging with journaling
[ Update screen before/after — drop export here ]

The current update screen is purely transactional — a page input, a radio button, and an optional text field that reads as an afterthought. The redesign reframes the same interaction as a reading ritual: a tactile stepper for page input, the book's current progress bar as context, and a journaling field labelled "What did you think?" with a private toggle and placeholder copy that invites reflection rather than demanding it. Updating progress stops feeling like admin.

04

Design decisions

Choices worth explaining

Each decision reflects a deliberate tension — between what the product was optimising for and what users were actually returning for.

  • Discovery and social features moved below the fold, not removed
    Goodreads' social layer has value — but leading with it misrepresents why most users open the app. Demoting, not deleting, preserves discoverability without making it the first thing readers have to navigate past.
  • Add Books surfaced inside the shelf, not behind a separate flow
    High-frequency tasks should require the fewest steps possible. Placing the action where users already are — inside the shelf — removes a navigation detour that users repeatedly complained about.
  • Journaling is optional, private by default
    Making reflection feel safe encourages use. A mandatory or public-first feature would read as social pressure — the opposite of what reading-tracking users want from a log.
  • Personalisation based on user activity, not algorithmic cold-start
    Recommendations are only useful when they feel relevant. The redesigned homepage tailors content to what the user is actively reading and recently shelved — not a generic "readers also liked" feed.
05

Business impact

Improving relevance, reducing friction, and deepening engagement

As a speculative concept, impact is framed as a hypothesis — grounded in the documented frustrations the redesign directly addresses.

↑ Relevance
Context-aware homepage surfaces content matched to each user's active reading — not a generic social feed
↓ Friction
Quick-add and inline shelf actions reduce the steps between intent and completion for the most frequent in-app tasks
↑ Retention
Progress logging paired with journaling creates a daily habit loop — giving users a reason to return beyond simply finishing a book
